GAN is a neural-network architecture used for Generative Modeling. The Generative Models are used to generate new ‘made-up/fake’ examples that’d appear to have come from the authentic distribution of samples. GANs are trained using two separate networks, a Generator, and a Discriminator, competing like adversaries. The Discriminator’s job is to detect the actual image from the dataset vs. the fake image generated by the trained Generator.

Have a look at GAN Original Paper for more details.

Our goal in this project is to build a GAN and train it over the MNIST dataset so that the network learns to generate fake MNIST digit images that would appear to have come from the real MNIST dataset. Still, in reality, these images are not part of the MNIST dataset.

GANs can be applied to several exciting applications…

  • Generate Realistic Photographs that are practically indistinguishable from an actual photograph as described in Large Scale GAN Training for High Fidelity Natural Image Synthesis paper.

  • Generate cartoon characters, as described in Towards the Automatic Anime Characters Creation with Generative Adversarial Networks paper.

  • Photos to Emojis translate from photographs of celebrities to emojis or small cartoon faces.

  • StackGAN generates 256x256 photo-realistic images conditioned on text descriptions. For example, if a description of a bird is provided, it will be able to generate an image of the bird based on the description; this will be an imaginary picture of a bird that does not exist in real words but looks real.

  • iGAN, based on user-drawn simple sketches, iGAN could produce photo-realistic samples that best matches the user sketch in real-time.

  • CartoonGAN, CartoonGAN can transform the photos of real-world scenes into cartoon style images. For example, it can take a picture of a natural person’s face and produce a catoonized image of that face.

  • CycleGAN, CycleGAN performs an image-to-image translation. Model trains in an unsupervised manner using a collection of images from the source and target domain that do not need to be related in any way. Models trains without any paired examples. For example, it can take a photo of a summer landscape and transform the same image into a winter landscape.

  • GAN is built by defining two networks…

    • Discriminator network: It’s a typical binary classifier. In our case, it accepts 784 (28x28) inputs and produces a single logit output that’s used to classify the input image as real (1) or fake (0). The network comprises fully-connected Linear layers with LeakyReLU activation. A dropout of 30% is applied after each linear layer except the last one.

    • Generator network: Consumes a noise vector (z of length 100) and up-samples it by passing it through various layers of the network. The network comprises fully-connected Linear layers with LeakyReLU activation. A dropout of 30% is applied after each linear layer except the last one. The final linear layer’s output is then passed through the tanh function to produce the final output of the Generator between -1 and 1.

  • Note that, unlike other supervised learning tasks, we can’t measure the performance of our GANs using a metric like accuracy. Performance measurement, in this case, would be Qualitative (human evaluation) rather than Quantitative.

  • Loss Function: GAN training is slightly different from our regular supervised neural-network training. In the case of GAN, two separate networks are being trained together, and networks have different and opposing goals (i.e., they are competing). The Discriminator is trying to identify if the image sample is genuine or its fake images generated by our Generator. Note that here we are NOT interested in classifying the MNIST digits themselves. Instead, two-loss functions are defined…

    1. real_loss: calculates loss when images are drawn from the actual dataset. The predicted output is compared against the target label 1, indicating authentic images.
    2. fake_loss: calculates loss when images are generated by the Generator. The predicted output is compared against the target label 0, indicating fake images.

  • Since we are training two separate networks, we need two separate optimizers for each network. In both cases, we use the Adam optimizer with a learning rate of 0.002.

  • Since classification is between two classes (real and fake) and our Discriminator outputs a logit, we use BCEWithLogitsLoss as the loss function.

  • During training, the Generator consumes a batch noise data (z) drawn from a normal distribution between range -1 and 1 and generates a batch of fake images

  • The Discriminator trains by consuming the actual MNIST and fake images and classifying real Vs. fake images

  • Discriminator and Generator compute their respective losses and improve for every subsequent epoch via backpropagation.

  • The main aim of the GAN training is to attain equilibrium between the competing networks. Equilibrium is the fundamental idea behind GANs, drawn from The Game Theory, which suggests that competing rational agents will ultimately reach an equilibrium where they can’t improve anymore. This is the optimal solution.

    The above plot does not look like a typical neural-network training loss plot. There are huge fluctuations at the beginning, and it’s very shaky after that.
